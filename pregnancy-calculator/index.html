Prenatal Care: Timing of Episodes & Laboratory Testing

Extracted from prenatal care guideline document

1. Timing of Prenatal Care Episodes

Preconception / Early Screening

  • A preconception visit is recommended for all people planning pregnancy.
  • Desire for pregnancy should be assessed during routine preventive health visits for ages 15–45.
  • Since roughly half of all pregnancies are unplanned, preconception content is often addressed at the first prenatal visit instead of a dedicated preconception visit.

2. Laboratory Testing

Preconception / First-Visit Labs

  • Infectious disease & immunization screening: rubella, tuberculosis, HIV, hepatitis B, varicella, herpes, hepatitis C, Zika, COVID.
  • Retested during pregnancy (even if done preconception): type and screen, HIV, hepatitis B, RPR (syphilis), gonorrhea, chlamydia.
  • Genetic carrier screening:
    • Cystic fibrosis and spinal muscular atrophy — offered to all patients.
    • Additional testing (e.g., sickle cell, Tay-Sachs) based on family history and race/ethnicity probabilities.
    • Available via referral to genetic counselors or screening tools (e.g., Horizon™).

Gestational Diabetes (GDM)

Insulin physiology note: Insulin needs can decrease in early pregnancy but rise across the second and third trimesters as insulin resistance increases. Insulin resistance can drop rapidly postpartum.

Screening/Diagnostic Tests
  • Outpatient screening: checked in the first trimester for at-risk populations, and/or between 24–28 weeks gestational age.
  • Inpatient: screening checked in all hospitalized patients who have not had a test outpatient during this pregnancy, by 28 weeks.
  • Recent medications (e.g., steroids) should be factored into interpretation of OGTT results.
  • Hemoglobin A1c — considered in all hospitalized patients with known diabetes mellitus who haven't had a measurement in the last 3 months, and when a diagnosis of diabetes is being considered.
Two-Step Strategy (most commonly used in the U.S.)
Step 1 — 50-g Glucose Challenge Test (GCT)
  • Administered in a nonfasting state.
  • Plasma glucose measured at 1 hour.
  • Positive screen defined as 1-hour glucose ≥135 mg/dL (institutional threshold); those who screen positive proceed to Step 2.
Step 2 — 3-Hour 100-g Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT)
  • Performed in the morning after an overnight fast of at least 8 hours.
  • 100 g oral glucose load administered.
  • Plasma glucose measured fasting, and at 1, 2, and 3 hours.
  • GDM diagnosed when at least 2 of 4 values are met or exceeded (Carpenter–Coustan criteria):

  • ACOG also supports an alternative, higher-threshold criteria set (National Diabetes Data Group): fasting ≥105, 1h ≥190, 2h ≥165, 3h ≥145 mg/dL — though the ADA favors the lower Carpenter–Coustan thresholds.
  • ACOG additionally notes that one elevated value (rather than two) may be used for diagnosis, though this increases GDM incidence.
Treatment — Glycemic Goals

Apply to both pre-existing and gestational diabetes.

  • Blood glucose < 90 mg/dL fasting for patients on pharmacotherapy (consider < 95 mg/dL if diet-managed).
  • Blood glucose < 100 mg/dL before meals.
  • Blood glucose < 120 mg/dL two hours post-prandial.
Surveillance after diagnosis (from Table 3, Section 1)
  • GDM requiring medication: twice-weekly NST/AFI testing starting at 32 weeks.
  • GDM requiring medication is also an indication for high-risk consultation/referral (Table 4).

Areas of Responsibility: Obstetric providers.
